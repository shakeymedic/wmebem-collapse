The Collapsed Patient

A Guideline-Based Approach for the UK Emergency Department

Red Flags

The Clinical Challenge

A patient presenting with collapse or Transient Loss of Consciousness (TLoC) is one of the most common and diagnostically challenging scenarios in the ED. The key task is to systematically differentiate benign causes from potentially life-threatening conditions.

Collapse

Abrupt loss of postural tone, with or without loss of consciousness.

TLoC ('Blackout')

Spontaneous loss of consciousness with complete recovery and no residual deficit.

Syncope

TLoC resulting from transient global cerebral hypoperfusion. The most common cause of TLoC.

Structured Initial Assessment

A meticulous assessment is the most powerful diagnostic tool. A thorough history, focused exam, and 12-lead ECG are often sufficient to establish a diagnosis.

1. Clinical History: Before, During & After

Crucially, obtain history from the patient and any witnesses (phone them!). Pre-hospital paramedic records are vital.

Before (Prodrome & Context)

  • Circumstances: Prolonged standing? During exertion (RED FLAG)? Situational trigger (cough, micturition)?
  • Posture: Supine, sitting, or standing? TLoC while lying down is a RED FLAG.
  • Prodromal Symptoms: Autonomic (nausea, sweat, warm) suggests vasovagal. Palpitations/chest pain suggests cardiac. Neurological 'aura' suggests seizure.

During (The Event)

  • Appearance: Pallor (syncope) vs. central cyanosis (seizure).
  • Movements: Brief, myoclonic jerks can occur in syncope. Rhythmic, tonic-clonic movements suggest seizure.
  • Tongue Biting: Side of tongue is highly specific for seizure. Tip of tongue is non-specific.
  • Duration: Syncope is typically < 1 minute. Seizures can be longer.

After (Recovery)

  • Confusion: Rapid, complete recovery suggests syncope. Prolonged post-ictal phase (confusion, drowsiness) strongly suggests seizure.
  • Focal Neurology: Any residual weakness (e.g., Todd's paresis) is a significant finding.

2. Focused Physical Examination

  • Core Components: Full vital signs, thorough cardiovascular exam, and focused neurological exam.
  • Cardiovascular Assessment: Actively search for signs of structural heart disease. Listen for murmurs (e.g., aortic stenosis), check for signs of heart failure (raised JVP, oedema, crackles).
  • Orthostatic Blood Pressure: Measure BP/HR after 5 mins supine, then after 3 mins standing. A sustained systolic drop of ≥20mmHg or diastolic drop of ≥10mmHg is diagnostic of orthostatic hypotension.

3. The Mandatory 12-Lead ECG

The ECG is mandatory for every patient with TLoC. It provides static clues to intermittent but life-threatening conditions. Any abnormality from the list below is a RED FLAG and requires urgent cardiology assessment.

  • Complete RBBB or LBBB
  • Any degree of AV Block (Mobitz II, 3rd degree)
  • Bifascicular Block
  • Ventricular arrhythmias (e.g. VT)
  • Inappropriate sinus bradycardia (<40bpm)
  • Long QT (>450ms) or Short QT (<350ms)
  • Brugada Syndrome pattern (coved ST in V1-V3)
  • Ventricular pre-excitation (WPW)
  • Pathological Q waves
  • LVH/RVH, T wave inversion (e.g. in ARVC)

Neurally Mediated (Reflex)

  • Vasovagal Syncope: "Common faint". Look for the "Three P's":
    • Posture: Prolonged standing/sitting.
    • Provoking factors: Pain, fear, distress.
    • Prodromal symptoms: Nausea, sweating etc.
  • Situational Syncope: Clear trigger (cough, micturition, swallow).
  • Carotid Sinus Syncope: Older adults, triggered by head turning, shaving, tight collars.

Orthostatic Hypotension (OH)

  • Common in older adults and those on multiple medications.
  • Diagnosed with history of TLoC on standing + positive orthostatic BP measurement.
  • Common Causes:
    • Volume depletion (dehydration, haemorrhage).
    • Medication-induced (antihypertensives, nitrates).
    • Autonomic Failure (e.g., Parkinson's disease).

Cardiac Syncope (High-Risk)

  • Most serious form, high mortality. Identify by red flags.
  • Arrhythmic: Abrupt onset, little/no prodrome (e.g., AV block, VT).
  • Structural/Cardiopulmonary:
    • Aortic Stenosis (exertional).
    • HOCM (exertional, family history).
    • Pulmonary Embolism (can be sole feature).
    • Aortic Dissection (severe tearing pain).

NICE Red Flags for Urgent Admission

The presence of ANY of these features mandates, at minimum, an urgent specialist cardiovascular assessment within 24 hours. In the ED, this usually means admission for monitoring and investigation.

History

  • ➤TLoC during exertion.
  • ➤TLoC while supine.
  • ➤Little or no warning prodrome.
  • ➤Preceded by chest pain or palpitations.
  • ➤New or unexplained breathlessness.
  • ➤Family history of sudden cardiac death <40 years.

Examination

  • ➤New or unexplained heart murmur.
  • ➤Clinical signs of heart failure.
  • ➤Persistent systolic BP <90 mmHg.
  • ➤Suspicion of significant GI bleed.

ECG

  • ➤Any conduction abnormality (BBB, AV Block).
  • ➤Arrhythmias or evidence of ischaemia.
  • ➤Long/Short QT, Brugada, WPW.
  • ➤Evidence of structural disease (LVH, Q waves).

Judicious Investigations

A targeted, "clinical suspicion-first" approach is high-yield. Avoid a routine "shotgun" approach.

  • Mandatory: Capillary blood glucose in ALL patients with TLoC or altered mental state.
  • Bloods (Selective):
    • FBC: Suspected anaemia or bleeding.
    • U&E: Dehydration, diuretic use, suspected electrolyte disturbance.
    • Troponin: ONLY if clinical picture suggests ACS.
  • Imaging (Highly Selective):
    • CT Head: NOT routine for syncope. Indicated for secondary head injury (per NICE guidelines) or primary intracranial catastrophe (e.g., SAH).
    • Echocardiography: Indicated if exam/ECG suggests structural cause (new murmur, heart failure signs, ECG changes).

Managing Emergencies

For the minority who are unstable, rapid intervention guided by standardised algorithms is key.

Unstable Tachyarrhythmia: Synchronised DC Cardioversion

Immediate electrical therapy if adverse signs (Shock, Syncope, Myocardial Ischaemia, Heart Failure). Requires procedural sedation.

Unstable Bradyarrhythmia: Atropine & Pacing

Atropine 500mcg IV (max 3mg). If ineffective, transcutaneous pacing or isoprenaline/adrenaline infusion as a bridge to definitive treatment.

Status Epilepticus (>5 mins): Benzodiazepines then AEDs

First line: Lorazepam 4mg IV or Midazolam 10mg buccal. Second line: Levetiracetam, Phenytoin, or Valproate. Escalate to ICU for refractory cases.

Unconscious Hypoglycaemia: IV Glucose / IM Glucagon

IV Glucose (e.g., 75ml 20%) is treatment of choice. If no IV access, Glucagon 1mg IM. Admit if caused by long-acting oral hypoglycaemic agents (e.g., Gliclazide).

Disposition & Safe Discharge

Safe to Discharge

Consider for discharge if a confident low-risk diagnosis is made AND there are no high-risk features present.

  • Uncomplicated Faint (Vasovagal): Clear "Three P's", normal exam, normal ECG.
  • Situational Syncope: Clear, consistent situational trigger.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Clear cause identified (e.g., dehydration) which can be addressed, and patient is stable.

Mandatory Discharge Advice

Safe discharge requires comprehensive education and safety-netting.

  • Education: Explain the mechanism of benign conditions. Advise on trigger avoidance and counter-pressure manoeuvres for vasovagal syncope.
  • Safety-Netting: Instruct patient to return immediately if they have TLoC with exertion, chest pain, shortness of breath, or severe headache.
  • Driving Restrictions: THIS IS A MANDATORY COMPONENT. Advise patient of their legal responsibilities according to current DVLA guidelines. An unexplained TLoC requires a period off driving.
